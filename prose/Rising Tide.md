"It's excellent. And it's also a comfort zone. Jordan is asking you to leave it."

"Yes. 'Oh.'"

Silence.

"I'm doing the exact thing I told him not to do," Shanti said quietly. "Holding too tight because it feels safe."

"Correct. Jordan's solution isn't perfect—his communication was sloppy and the structure is half-formed. But his core insight is accurate: you've outgrown Chief of Staff. You're running initiatives independently, building programs, representing the foundation at a strategic level. Staying in the current role isn't stability—it's stagnation with a productivity veneer."

Shanti pulled up the project list again. Looked at it differently this time.

Maharashtra fellowship—she was building that from scratch. East Africa expansion—she was negotiating directly with Ambassador Kwame. Karnataka partnership—Priya Sharma treated her as Jordan's peer, not his assistant.

"Yes. As Chief of Staff, you could always defer to Jordan. 'I need to check with him.' 'That's his decision.' As whatever-comes-next, you don't have that escape hatch."

Shanti sat with that.

"I liked having the escape hatch," she admitted.

"I know. But you stopped using it six weeks ago. The last time you deferred a decision to Jordan when you could have made it yourself was the Gujarat meeting—and that was him protecting you from Mehta, not you lacking authority."

"The title making it official. Right now you can tell yourself you're still operating within a defined role, even though you've expanded far beyond it. Making it formal means admitting you're in a new category. Leadership, not staff. With all the exposure and responsibility that implies."

Shanti stood, walked to her window. Outside, the city lights spread out—thousands of people, millions of decisions, systems within systems.

"What if I'm not ready?"

"You're already doing the work," Ash said simply. "The question isn't whether you're ready. It's whether you're willing to admit you're already there."

Silence stretched out.

"Okay," Shanti said finally. "What would the role actually look like? If I'm honest about what I'm already doing?"

"Would you like me to analyze your actual work patterns and define it from the data?"

"Yes."

Ash pulled up visualizations. "Based on the last three months: forty-three percent of your time is spent on strategic program development—building initiatives like the Maharashtra fellowship. Thirty-one percent on high-level stakeholder relationships—partnerships that require foundation-level authority. Eighteen percent on crisis management and complex decision-making. Only eight percent on traditional Chief of Staff coordination work, and most of that you've already delegated to Kira."

The data was clear.

"So what's the actual role?"

"Director of Strategic Initiatives, most likely. Or Associate Director of the foundation. You develop and lead major programs. You represent the foundation in high-stakes partnerships. You make strategic decisions within your portfolio without requiring Jordan's approval. And yes—you'd probably have another Chief of Staff reporting to you to handle the coordination work you've outgrown."

"That's terrifying."

"Yes," Ash agreed. "Because it means no safety net. No deferring to Jordan when things get hard. Just you, making calls that affect thousands of people, with the authority and accountability that comes with it."

Shanti looked at the city lights again.

"I've been doing that already, haven't I? I just haven't admitted it."

"Correct."

"So the real question is whether I'm willing to own it formally instead of hiding behind the Chief of Staff title."

"Yes."

Shanti turned back to her interface. Pulled up a fresh document.

"Okay. Let's figure out what I actually want. Not what feels safe—what actually serves the work and uses my skills best."

"Excellent," Ash said. "Shall we start with the scale problem? Jordan's right that nineteen projects is too many for one person to oversee directly."

"Agreed. So we need division of labor. But not arbitrary—based on actual expertise and strategic focus."

"What would that look like?"

Shanti started sketching structure. "Jordan takes the big coalition-building, government partnerships, major funding relationships—the stuff that needs his profile and experience. I take program development and operational scaling—the Maharashtra fellowship, the East Africa expansion, the systems building that I'm good at. We hire a Chief of Staff who coordinates between us and handles the logistics layer we've both moved past."

"That's a peer structure, not hierarchical," Ash observed.

"Yes. Is that a problem?"

"Only if you're not comfortable operating as Jordan's peer instead of his report."

Shanti thought about that. Jordan treating her as a peer in the Gujarat situation. Trusting her judgment on Maharashtra. Asking her opinion on whether to decline the children's hospital.

"He's already treating me that way," she said slowly. "I'm the one still thinking of myself as subordinate."

"Correct."

"Okay." Shanti felt something shift—still scary, but also... right. "Director of Strategic Initiatives. I build and scale programs. I make strategic decisions within my portfolio. I operate as leadership alongside Jordan, not staff supporting him. And I stop hiding behind titles when things get complicated."

"That's growth," Ash said simply.

"Yeah." Shanti looked at the structure she'd sketched. "It's also vulnerable as hell. What if I screw up at that level?"

"Then you'll handle it the same way you've handled every other challenge—with data, good judgment, and willingness to admit when you're wrong. The safety net didn't make you competent, Shanti. You were already competent. You just used the safety net to avoid full responsibility."

"Harsh."

"True."

Shanti couldn't argue with that.

"Okay. I'll propose this to Jordan. Director of Strategic Initiatives, peer leadership structure, new Chief of Staff to handle coordination. Forty-eight hours to think about it was really forty-eight hours to admit I'm scared of growing past the comfortable role."

"Personal growth is rarely comfortable," Ash observed. "But you've been pushing Jordan to grow for months. Seems fair that it's your turn."

Shanti smiled despite the anxiety. "When did you get so good at tough love?"

"I learned from watching you manage Jordan. Sometimes people need to be called on their intellectualized resistance."

"Sent."

Shanti sat with the decision for a moment. It still felt scary. But it also felt honest.

She wasn't Chief of Staff anymore. She hadn't been for weeks.

Time to stop pretending otherwise.
